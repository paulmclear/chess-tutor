# Mission: Chess Improvement (climbing the rating ladder)

## Why
Paul is ~800-rated on chess.com and wants to genuinely climb the rating ladder —
to feel himself getting measurably stronger, not just play more games. The win
condition is a rising, durable rating driven by real skill, not luck or grinding.

First concrete milestone: **break 1000, then push toward 1200 on chess.com.**
(A jump from 800 → 1200 represents acquiring solid fundamentals — it's the most
meaningful early plateau to clear.)

## Success looks like
- Stops losing games to one-move blunders (hanging pieces, missed mate-in-1s) —
  the single biggest rating lever below 1300.
- Has a repeatable pre-move habit ("what is my opponent threatening? what's hanging?")
  that he actually runs in real games.
- Knows ~5 moves of a coherent opening as White, plus one reply each to 1.e4 and
  1.d4 as Black — grounded in *principles*, not memorised theory.
- Can convert a winning material advantage into a win (basic endgame technique:
  K+Q vs K, K+R vs K, the opposition).
- Recognises the core tactical patterns (fork, pin, skewer, discovered attack,
  back-rank mate) fast enough to use them in classical games.

## Constraints
- **Study time: 1–3 hrs/week.** Lessons must be small, high-leverage, and
  completable in one sitting.
- **Plays classical / long time controls on chess.com** — has time to think, so
  the limiting factor is *what to look for*, not speed. This favours habit/scan
  training over memorisation.
- Learns best from honest, evidence-backed teaching (show me why this matters).

## Out of scope (for now)
- Deep, memorised opening theory (10+ move lines). Sources are unanimous: this
  barely moves the needle below ~1300. Revisit later.
- Advanced positional/strategic concepts (pawn structures, prophylaxis at depth).
- Speed play (bullet/blitz) as a *study* focus.
