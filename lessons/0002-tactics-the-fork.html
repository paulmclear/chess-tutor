Chess · Lesson 02 · Tactics

Winning Material — the Fork

Lesson 1 stopped you giving pieces away. Now you start taking them — on purpose.

⏱ One sitting · ~20 min 🎯 Mission: 800 → 1200 ♞ Pattern: the fork

A tactic is a short, forcing sequence that wins material (or mates) by force — your opponent can't stop it, only minimise the damage.

Where this fits

In Lesson 1 you learned the blunder-check — Step 2 was "scan for loose pieces." Tactics are the flip side: once you can see loose pieces and exposed kings, you can set up positions that win them by force. Tactics decide the overwhelming majority of games below ~1500 (Chessable), so this is where your points are.

1The five patterns — your toolkit

Almost every tactic you'll ever play is one of these five (or a combination). Today we learn the vocabulary, then master the first. Keep the Tactical Patterns reference card handy.

  • ♞Fork Today
    One piece attacks two (or more) targets at once. Opponent saves one — you take the other.
  • 📌Pin
    A piece can't move because a more valuable piece sits behind it. It's stuck — so attack it.
  • 🍢Skewer
    Like a pin reversed: the valuable piece is in front, forced to move, exposing the one behind.
  • 💥Discovered attack
    Move one piece to unleash an attack from the piece behind it. Two threats from one move.
  • ♜Back-rank mate
    A king trapped behind its own pawns, mated by a rook/queen on the back row.

2The fork — why it's your best friend

A fork attacks two things at once. Your opponent has one move; they can only rescue one. You collect the other. The knight is the king of forks for two reasons:

  • A knight check can't be blocked. If a knight forks your king and your queen, you must move the king out of check — and the queen drops. No interposing a piece.
  • Knights attack squares of the opposite colour to where they stand and jump over pieces — so they hit targets that "feel" far apart and undefended.

A fork that hits the king and the queen has a name: the royal fork. But pawns, bishops, rooks and queens all fork too — anything that attacks two loose targets at once.

How to hunt forks: look for two enemy targets (king, queen, rook, or any undefended piece) and ask — "is there a single square my knight/pawn/piece could land on that hits both?" Exposed kings and loose pieces are fork-bait. This is just Lesson 1's loose-piece scan, pointed at the attack.

3Train it — find the fork

White to move in each. Find the move that forks two targets. Decide before you click.

Puzzle 1 · knight fork

White to move. There's a check that wins material — find it.

Your knight is on b5. Which square hits the king and something else?

Puzzle 2 · the royal fork

White to move. Win the queen.

Knight on d5. Find the square that checks the king on g8 and also hits c8.

Puzzle 3 · the humble pawn fork

White to move. A pawn can win a piece — how?

Both Black knights sit on the same rank. Is there one push that attacks both?

Answer the three puzzles above.

4Your reps this week

  • Themed puzzles: on Lichess you can drill one pattern at a time — lichess.org/training/fork. Do 10–15 fork puzzles; the geometry starts to jump out at you.
  • In your games: every move, after the blunder-check, add one question — "can I fork anything?" Look for enemy pieces (especially king + queen/rook) a single knight-hop or pawn-push apart.
  • Watch for the royal fork whenever the enemy king and queen are both out and undefended. It's the most satisfying point you'll ever win.
