The Blunder-Check

The thirty-second habit that wins more games at your level than any opening ever will.

⏱ One sitting · ~20 min 🎯 Mission: 800 → 1200 ♟ For classical games

You asked about openings and middlegame theory — and we'll get there. But first, the uncomfortable, evidence-backed truth about your rating.

Why this lesson, first

Below ~1300, almost every decisive game is decided by a one-move blunder — a piece left hanging, or a missed capture/mate. Not by opening knowledge. Coaches and trainers are unanimous (Chess Tournament Guide, "Training Plan Under 1000"; Chessable, "Opening Theory for Beginners").

You play classical / long time controls. That is your superpower: you have the clock to run a simple checklist every move. Most 800-rated players lose not because they can't see the threat — but because they never looked. This lesson installs the looking.

1The four-step scan

Before every move, run this. It takes seconds once it's a habit. IM John Bartholomew calls the underlying idea building a "sense of danger" by actively scanning for loose pieces (Chess Fundamentals #1: Undefended Pieces).

  1. What did my opponent's last move do? Their move always has a point. Does it attack one of my pieces, threaten a capture, or set up a check/mate? Find the point before you do anything else.
  2. Scan for loose pieces — both sides. Sweep the board: is any piece (mine or theirs) attacked and not defended enough? Loose pieces drop. Theirs are free material for me; mine are emergencies.
  3. Now pick my move — then re-check it. Imagine the move played. Does it leave anything of mine hanging? Does it walk into a check or fork? This single re-check kills most blunders.
  4. Look at all checks & captures. Every check and every capture — mine and theirs. Forcing moves are where tactics live. If you only ever scan these, you'll already outplay most of your bracket.

Shorthand to mutter to yourself each move:
“What's their threat? · What's loose? · Does my move hang anything? · Checks & captures?”

2Train it — three positions

Reading the checklist does nothing. Running it does. For each position below, decide your answer before you click — then get instant feedback. Boards are shown from White's side (rank 1 at the bottom).

Puzzle 1 · Step 2 — loose pieces

It's White to move. Is there free material to grab?

Scan Black's pieces. Is anything attacked and undefended?

Puzzle 2 · Step 1 — their threat

Black has just played …Bg4. It's White to move. What is the threat?

Ask the Step 1 question: what does that bishop move do?

Puzzle 3 · Step 3 — check your own move

You're tempted to play Bxf7+ ("a check, and I grab a pawn!"). Good move?

Before you commit: what defends f7? Calculate the recapture.

Answer the three puzzles above.

3Your reps this week

Knowledge fades; habits stick through reps. In your 1–3 hours:

  • Every game, every move: run the four-step scan. Slowly and deliberately — you have the clock. It will feel laborious for ~10 games, then it goes automatic.
  • 10–15 puzzles, a few times this week on Lichess Puzzles (free, unlimited). Don't rush — the goal is seeing, not speed.
  • After each loss, find the move it turned on. 90% of the time it'll be a hanging piece or a missed capture — exactly what this scan catches. That's the proof.
