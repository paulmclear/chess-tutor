Tactical Patterns

The five ways to win material by force. Nearly every tactic is one of these — or a mix.

♞

Fork

One piece attacks two targets at once; the opponent saves one, you take the other. Knights are the best forkers — a knight check can't be blocked. King + queen = the “royal fork”.

📌

Pin

A piece can't (or shouldn't) move because a more valuable piece sits behind it on the same line. It's frozen — so attack it again, often with a pawn.

🍢

Skewer

A pin in reverse: the valuable piece is in front, forced to move, exposing the lesser piece behind it. Hit a king or queen on an open line and grab what's behind.

💥

Discovered attack

Move one piece to unleash an attack from the piece behind it — two threats from one move. A “discovered check” is brutal: the moving piece can grab anything while the king deals with check.

♜

Back-rank mate

A king trapped behind its own un-moved pawns, mated by a rook or queen sliding onto the back row. Give your own king “luft” (a pawn move for air) to avoid it.

How to hunt tactics: after your blunder-check, scan for enemy loose pieces and an exposed king. Then ask: is there a single move that hits two of them (fork), lines two up (pin / skewer), or unmasks an attacker (discovery)? Tactics live on checks, captures, and undefended pieces.
